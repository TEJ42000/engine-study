## PART A — ENGINES

### Engine: System of government classification
- Gate: State the substantive criterion before touching the facts — does the chief executive have their own democratic mandate, or depend on parliamentary confidence?
- Steps:
  1. State the criterion (mandate vs confidence).
  2. Locate the chief executive (which article vests executive authority, who heads it).
  3. Check the head of state's mandate (directly elected = independent mandate; elected by parliament = none).
  4. Check confidence dependency (is there a vote-of-no-confidence / authorisation-by-parliament mechanism?).
  5. Explicitly rule out the wrong alternatives (esp. rule out semi-presidential by showing no two-headed executive / no directly elected president).
  6. Name the system + a comparator country.
- Trigger: Question hands you constitutional articles and asks "parliamentary, presidential or semi-presidential?"
- Satellites: parliamentary vs proportional representation (electoral-system confusion); ceremonial vs executive president; two-headed executive test; comparator anchors (Estonia/Germany parliamentary, France semi-presidential, US presidential).
- Maturity reached: Reflex. This was the single biggest early weakness (wrong on both Mock 1 Q1 and Mock 2 Q1) and by exam eve it retrieved 100% across all five countries on the comparative grid and cold recall.

### Engine: Federalism vs unitarism / anti-commandeering
- Gate: Identify the load-bearing concept — state sovereignty — before describing structure.
- Steps:
  1. Lay out the three-tier structure (federal / state / municipal).
  2. Establish that states are sovereign in federal systems with constitutionally guaranteed prerogatives; municipalities are not sovereign in either system.
  3. Apply the bypass logic (federal directly commanding municipalities would bypass the sovereign state layer and disrupt the federal balance).
  4. Contrast unitary (sub-national units have no constitutionally guaranteed prerogatives → nothing to protect).
  5. Distinguish sovereignty from autonomy where relevant.
- Trigger: "Why does [doctrine] fit a federal state more than a unitary state?" or any federal/unitary comparison.
- Satellites: dual vs cooperative federalism (US federal executes vs Germany Art 83 GG Länder execute); decentralisation / deconcentration / devolution; centripetal vs centrifugal; the comparative grid rows (sovereignty, constitutions, bicameralism, review of division, etc.).
- Maturity reached: Stable. Went from 1.5–2/5 to 4–4.5/5 after one gap-closure block; state-sovereignty concept was named cleanly on cold recall.

### Engine: Constitutional review classification + efficacy
- Gate: Classify along the dimensions before evaluating anything.
- Steps:
  1. Time: ex ante vs ex post.
  2. Scope: concrete (actual case required) vs abstract (general principles, no case).
  3. (Concentration: concentrated vs diffuse; Form: the output/mechanism — secondary dimensions.)
  4. Justify each classification from the provision's wording.
  5. For efficacy: define efficacy = degree to which de jure (constitutional prescription) corresponds with de facto (actual behaviour) — Kokott & Kaspar — then link (ex ante prevents unconstitutional law entering the system, so de facto can't drift from de jure).
- Trigger: Provision describing a review power; "ex post or ex ante / abstract or concrete?"; any question using the word "efficacy."
- Satellites: the four dimensions of review; efficacy as de jure/de facto correspondence; the "Form" dimension (frequently mislabelled as concentration).
- Maturity reached: Stable, with one soft spot. Part (a) classification was reflex; efficacy definition was solid Sunday (1.5–2/2 on rewrite) but blanked once on cold recall two days later ("IDK") before being re-locked. Retrieval-fragile under fatigue but comprehension solid.

### Engine: ECHR substantive limitation framework
- Gate: Establish the right is a qualified right (so limitation analysis applies) before running the test.
- Steps:
  1. Prescribed by law (basis in national law, accessible, foreseeable).
  2. Legitimate aim (must fit the exhaustive list in the relevant 2nd paragraph).
  3. Necessary in a democratic society (proportionality + subsidiarity).
  4. Margin of appreciation overlay — narrow on the commitment, wide on means; political/watchdog speech attracts a very narrow margin.
  5. Conclude on permissibility.
- Trigger: Any limitation of a qualified ECHR right (expression, privacy, religion, assembly); a fact pattern with a statute restricting a right + a prosecuted individual.
- Satellites: margin of appreciation differentiated approach; principle of effectiveness (Airey) vs living instrument (Tyrer); public-figure doctrine; chilling effect; proportionality vs subsidiarity distinction.
- Maturity reached: Tested/stable. Student doubted this was even examinable, then applied it correctly to a fresh hypothetical (3.5–4/5) — the gap was depth on prong 3 and naming the margin, not the framework itself.

### Engine: Militant democracy / party bans
- Gate: Name the doctrine (Wehrhafte Demokratie) and the applicable model before applying a threshold.
- Steps:
  1. Name Wehrhafte Demokratie (self-defending democracy).
  2. Identify the model + threshold: militant (Germany, high) / institutional (NL, France, semi-high) / liberal-Brandenburg (US, UK, very high).
  3. Identify the banning authority (Germany: BVerfG at request of Bundestag/Bundesrat; France: executive+judicial via PPS; NL: civil courts via PPS; US: individual prosecution/RICO).
  4. Apply threshold to facts; for Germany, add the potentiality requirement (NPD 2017 — anti-constitutional aims are not enough; the party must have a realistic chance of achieving them).
  5. Use salient facts (e.g. polling %) as the hook for the potentiality analysis.
  6. Optional comparative contrast (how another model would treat the same facts).
- Trigger: A party/association facing a ban; any Art 21(2) GG-equivalent provision.
- Satellites: the three models with thresholds and authorities; the potentiality requirement; historical anchors (Weimar → Art 67; Enabling Act 1933 → Art 79(3)); party-ban placement in the comparative grid.
- Maturity reached: Tested → stable after correction. Got the France/NL models wrong on the comparative grid (called them "liberal"), corrected same day, then mapped all models+countries correctly on cold recall including the potentiality requirement.

### Engine: Democratic backsliding analysis
- Gate: Reach for the four-mechanism framework (Lecture 6B) as the structuring device, not an ad hoc argument.
- Steps:
  1. Define backsliding (gradual, often lawful erosion by populist actors; tilting the playing field; hollowing institutions).
  2. Deploy the four mechanisms: undermining judicial independence / legislation through invoking crises / targeting independent regulatory authorities / vagueness of norms.
  3. For defend-or-oppose framings, pick a side and use the framework to show what a single measure does and doesn't cover.
  4. Anchor with examples (Polish NJC Art 186(1)/187(4); 9-11, COVID, 2008; the three regulatory-capture framings; Polish Section 107(1) §1).
- Trigger: Any question on backsliding, entrenchment as a defence against it, or resilience of democratic institutions.
- Satellites: the four thinkers on sovereignty (Bodin/Hobbes/Locke/Rousseau); entrenchment types (procedural vs substantive super-entrenchment); the theorist spine (Müller anti-pluralism, Sadurski invisibility mechanisms, Scheppele unitary executive / counter-constitution, Davidsson diagonal recovery, Bošnjak interdependence).
- Maturity reached: Stable on content, retrieval-fragile on the list. The four mechanisms failed a flat cold recall (1/4), then came back 4/4 once hung on a narrative hook ("neuter courts → exploit crises → capture watchdogs → write vague laws").

### Engine: Domestic applicability of international law (monism/dualism)
- Gate: Ask how international law *applies* domestically, not which source ranks higher.
- Steps:
  1. Distinguish monism (international law applies as such, no transformation; constitution sets its rank) from dualism/transformism (must be transformed via national act; takes the rank of the incorporating act).
  2. Read the provision for what its *existence* implies (a hierarchy provision for treaties implies a monist system that needs to rank them).
  3. Avoid the trap of "constitution prevails → therefore dualist."
  4. Name the system (incl. "conditional monist" where the constitution sets the rank).
- Trigger: A provision about treaties/international agreements vs domestic law; "monist or dualist?"
- Satellites: conditional monism vs pure monism; NL Article 120 (prohibits review of Acts against the Constitution) + Arts 93–94 (direct effect of self-executing treaty provisions).
- Maturity reached: Stable/reflex. Clean on Mock 2 Q6 (~3.5–4/5) and clean on cold recall both times.

### Engine: Political-theory sovereignty (four thinkers)
- Gate: Answer the literal demand — WHO is the sovereign — before describing the theory.
- Steps:
  1. Name the thinker(s).
  2. State who each identifies as the sovereign: Bodin = monarch; Hobbes = absolute sovereign (monarch or assembly); Locke = the people (government as agent/trustee); Rousseau = the people collectively through the general will.
  3. Give the load-bearing distinguisher (Bodin: absolute+indivisible, no social contract; Hobbes: all rights transferred, no revolt; Locke: rights retained, right to revolt; Rousseau: general will inalienable, citizens co-authors).
- Trigger: A sovereignty quote + "which philosophers would support this / who is the sovereign?"; any compare-the-thinkers prompt.
- Satellites: pouvoir constituant vs constitué (Sieyès); why Locke doesn't fit an "absolute sovereignty" quote.
- Maturity reached: Reflex by exam eve. Rousseau specifically drifted toward a Lockean "delegation" framing under pressure on Mock 2 Q5 (2.5–3/5), was flagged and re-locked, and came back clean and drift-free on the final night's cold recall.

## PART B — MECHANICS

- Recurring error types:
  - **GATE_SKIP** — answering adjacent material instead of the question's literal demand. Hit system-classification (Q1: described semi-presidential traits without applying the criterion), sovereignty theory (Q5: described theories instead of naming who is sovereign), efficacy (Q4b: defined court fairness instead of running the de jure/de facto gate). This was the signature error of the course.
  - **WRONG_TOOL** — reaching for the adjacent-but-wrong concept. Unitary state vs unitary executive theory (Mock 1 Q4); parliamentary system vs proportional representation (Mock 1 Q2 / Mock 2 Q1); efficacy defined as procedural justice rather than the Kokott & Kaspar concept; backsliding answered with an ad hoc "other methods" argument instead of the four-mechanism framework.
  - **PRECISION** — right concept, wrong exact wording/label. The Airey phrase mangled repeatedly ("elusive"/"vague and elusive" for "theoretical and illusory"); the four review dimensions with "Form" mislabelled as concentration; France/NL party-ban model mislabelled "liberal"; Germany review mislabelled "abstract ex ante"; recurrent word-retrieval slips ("utilitarian" for "authoritarian," "dismeyed") tied to concussion fatigue.

- What fixed each error:
  - GATE_SKIP → the **literal-answer discipline** drill (write the operative demand at the top of scratch paper, answer it first) + marks-earning rewrites of the exact failed questions.
  - WRONG_TOOL → the **disambiguation-pairs sheet** (ten confused pairs, one line each side) + MCQ self-quizzes whose distractors were the student's own past confusions.
  - PRECISION → **hand-writing the exact phrase 3–5×**, cold recall tests (not MCQ — recognition hid these), and **narrative hooks** for lists (the four-mechanism story fixed the flat-list recall failure).

- The diagnosis loop:
  Cold timed mock → mark hard against model → extract *named* failure modes (not "weak on topic X") → 30-min gap-closure block per named failure (lock concept in own words → redo the exact question cold → re-mark) → confirm closed before moving on → second cold mock as fresh diagnostic → consolidate to one-page cheat sheet → cold-recall self-tests surface any survivors → drill only the survivors. Concretely: Mock 1 leaked system-classification, sovereignty, efficacy, literal-answer; those were drilled to closure; Mock 2 (cold, concussed) re-leaked the same four plus retrieval-fragility on lists; those were re-drilled; final cold recall (72%) leaked only the four mechanisms and efficacy, which were closed in one more cycle.

- What was distinct about THIS course:
  Almost entirely **applied, not memorization-heavy**. Content recall alone scored ~35%; the marks lived in *technique* — definitions-first, naming the precise doctrine, answering the literal demand, substantiating with case/article, addressing every clause, full word count, conclusion. The method flexed by making the **answer machinery** the primary object of drilling (rewrites and literal-answer discipline) rather than flashcard-style content cramming. It also had to flex hard around a **mild concussion** (days 3–7 of recovery): the two-hour cold mock was chunked into one-question-at-a-time sessions with breaks, feedback was moved inline (grade each question immediately rather than holding to the end), and pace became symptom-led rather than schedule-led — with the resit repeatedly offered as a genuine fallback.

- Highest-value move:
  **The marks-earning rewrite of cold-mock failures.** Redoing each failed question against the rubric, immediately after seeing where it bled, is what converted 35% into the high-70s/80s. It simultaneously closed the GATE_SKIP error (forced the literal answer), the WRONG_TOOL error (forced the correct named concept), and built the definitions-first machinery — all on the exact questions that had just exposed the gaps.
