## PART A — ENGINES

### Engine: Tort liability (Book VI 5-step)
- Gate: Anchor VI.–1:101 first (damage + causation + accountability → reparation); before writing the Issue, label the parties — who acted, who relied, who's trying to escape.
- Steps:
  1. Damage — legally relevant? (Ch 2: 2:201 injury / 2:202 third-person / 2:203 dignity / 2:206 property)
  2. Causation — but-for (4:101), analysed before accountability
  3. Accountability — stack both routes: fault (3:101 intention / 3:102 negligence / 3:103–104 children) AND no-fault (3:201–206)
  4. Defences (Ch 5) — name the gate and defeat it
  5. Reparation — 6:101 (as if damage never occurred)
- Trigger: harm/injury, no contract; "advise on route(s) to reparation."
- Satellites: six P's (3:201–206); contributory fault 5:102 (reduces, not bars); event beyond control 5:302; secondary victims 2:202; age ladder 3:103/104.
- Maturity reached: reflex. Ran cleanly on the mock (Q1 3.5/5); the old party-flip is fixed; only losses were article-number slips, not structure.

### Engine: Contract formation (Book II Ch 4)
- Gate: Is there a binding contract? Intention judged objectively (4:102).
- Steps: 4:101 intention + sufficient agreement → 4:102 objective test → 4:201 offer (vs invitation) → 4:202 revocation → 4:204 acceptance (silence ≠ acceptance) → 4:205 concluded when acceptance reaches offeror → 4:208 modified acceptance (material = counter-offer).
- Trigger: "is there a contract?"; informal/social arrangements; "I was only being friendly / never meant to be bound."
- Satellites: objective theory (Bank Guarantee); offer-to-the-world (Carlill); offer vs invitation to treat.
- Maturity reached: stable. Mock Q5 (Bank Guarantee) 4.5/5, no party-flip.

### Engine: Validity / defects of consent (Book II Ch 7)
- Gate: Can the party avoid? — and STACK all viable grounds, never pick one.
- Steps: mistake 7:201 (conditions in (1), bars in (2): inexcusable / risk assumed) · fraud 7:205 (disclosure factors in (3): expertise/cost/access/importance) · coercion 7:206 (wrongful threat; bar = reasonable alternative) · unfair exploitation 7:207 · effects (7:209 notice, 7:212 retrospective + restitution, 7:215 no exclusion for fraud/coercion/exploitation) · illegality (7:301 fundamental principles = auto-void / 7:302 mandatory rules = court chooses).
- Trigger: defective consent — mistake, expert staying silent, threat, exploitation of distress.
- Satellites: spot-the-expertise rule (who holds expertise flips the outcome); restitution/effects; illegality fork.
- Maturity reached: tested but leaky — the weakest engine. Mock Q2 2/5: selected 7:207 when the fact pattern was 7:205 fraud + 7:201 mistake, and failed to stack. Wrong-tool + gate-skip both fired here.

### Engine: Remedies (Book III Ch 3)
- Gate: Is the non-performance excused? (3:101 — excused NP bars specific performance + damages; only termination/withhold/price-reduction survive.)
- Steps: 3:1 gateway → 3:3 specific performance (301 money / 302 non-money + bars) → 3:4 withhold → 3:5 terminate (502 fundamental — two grounds / 503 Nachfrist / 504 anticipatory) → 3:6 price reduction → 3:7 damages (701 strict right → 702 measure "as if performed" → 703 foreseeability at conclusion → 704 contributory → 705 mitigation). Section number climbs 3→7 alongside the remedy.
- Trigger: breach/non-performance; "what can the creditor do?"
- Satellites: 3:502 two grounds (not four); cumulation 3:102; the 701→705 damages walk; strict liability 3:701 vs tort fault.
- Maturity reached: stable. 3:1→3:7 climb locked; a brief book/chapter mix-up (II.4 formation vs III.3 remedies) surfaced in taper and was patched. Mock Q3 3/5, docked for a fact inversion, not framework.

### Engine: Supervening-event fork
- Gate: FORK FIRST — is performance still possible, or truly impossible?
- Steps: still possible but ruinous → III.–1:110 hardship (conditions incl. (3)(d) genuine attempt to negotiate) → court varies/terminates. Truly impossible → III.–3:104 force majeure → excused.
- Trigger: event after conclusion disrupts performance — price spike, export ban, destruction, strike, cost overrun.
- Satellites: genus never perishes (generic goods stay on the hardship side); Davis Contractors (more onerous ≠ frustration); Bad Harvest (species can perish); EDF strike (external force majeure).
- Maturity reached: concept reflex, citation shaky — the persistent drifter. The possible/impossible logic was automatic, but the article numbers and book repeatedly slipped (1:101 for 1:110; Book VI/II for Book III; 3:104). Safeguarded by open-book lookup rather than fully internalised.

### Engine: Prescription
- Gate: identify the clock and whether it's suspended; answer every sub-issue asked.
- Steps: 7:201 three years → 7:203 runs from the act → 7:301 suspended while creditor can't reasonably know → 7:307 longstop 10 yrs / 30 for personal injury; then bolt on any defence the question adds.
- Trigger: time-bar; latent injury; "can they still claim?"
- Satellites: Howald Moor (discoverability); pairing with a second sub-issue (e.g. contributory fault 5:102).
- Maturity reached: tested/stable — drilled and the discoverability rule locked; not tested on the final mock.

### Engine: Interpretation
- Gate: subjective common intention first, reasonable-person fallback.
- Steps: 8:101 common intention → 8:103 contra proferentem → 9:101 implied terms.
- Trigger: ambiguous clause, especially an insurance exclusion.
- Satellites: Houghton v Trafalgar (contra proferentem).
- Maturity reached: drafted/tested — lighter coverage; the one case (Houghton) locked after a morning wobble.

### Engine: Case-law answer (closed-book)
- Gate: match the fact pattern to the ONE controlling case before writing.
- Steps: IRAC — Issue (name parties + legal question) → Rule (case + ratio, stating both the main rule AND its exception) → Application (map ratio explicitly onto the facts; ~40% of marks) → Conclusion.
- Trigger: any closed-book case question.
- Satellites: "one case only" discipline (a second case loses marks); verbatim phrase hooks on the high-value cases; trigger→case mapping table.
- Maturity reached: reflex. 13.5/15 on the final mock; cold recall clean at 5/6 with the single miss (Houghton) resealed inside a minute. This became the strong half.

## PART B — MECHANICS

- Recurring error types:
  - **PRECISION** (dominant): article-number transposition under fatigue — 3:104↔3:201, 5:302↔5:102, 7:207↔7:205, 1:101↔1:110, and book slips (Book IV/VI/II where Book III was correct). Also answer-length overruns (mock Q1 and Q3 ran 2–3× the 150-word cap). Hit tort accountability, tort defences, validity, and the supervening fork hardest.
  - **WRONG_TOOL**: choosing the wrong ground/article — 7:207 unfair exploitation for a 7:205 fraud pattern; 5:302 event-beyond-control for a 5:102 contributory-fault fact. Hit validity and tort defences.
  - **GATE_SKIP**: not stacking all grounds on validity; skipping a defence gate (force-majeure/5:302 on a storm question); one fact inversion (reading "no attempt to negotiate" as "notified immediately," which handed away the 1:110(3)(d) point). Rooted in not reading the facts twice.
- What fixed each error:
  - PRECISION → lookup practice (verify every number against the tabbed book before writing) + rapid-fire routing drills + the six P's mnemonic (People/Property/Pets/Products/Petrol/Poison = 201–206) + a start-of-exam brain-dump sheet with the drifters at the top.
  - WRONG_TOOL → rapid-fire trigger→article routing and the "spot-the-expertise" rule.
  - GATE_SKIP → the "stack every ground" rule, the "name and defeat the defence" rule, and "read the facts twice before the Issue line."
- The diagnosis loop: cold timed mock → map each miss to one of the seven examiner rules → drill the specific fix → reseal by recall. Mock Q1 leaked 3:104-for-3:201 and 5:302-for-5:102 → four-article drill → resealed. Taper-stage brain-dump-from-memory then surfaced three further gaps (a "parent" intrusion into the six P's, the II.4-vs-III.3 remedies mix-up, and crossed articles on the fork), each patched the same session and re-confirmed the next morning.
- What was distinct about THIS course: a two-mode exam — open-book DCFR application (half the marks) plus closed-book case recall (the other half) — so the method had to run two different games at once. It was applied, not pure memorisation: ~40% of case marks live in fact-application, and DCFR marks live in the sub-paragraphs, not article headings. The key flex was recognising the student's real risk was number-transposition under fatigue, not conceptual gaps — so for the open-book half the build was a tabbed, four-colour-highlighted DCFR + a verify-before-writing habit that turned the open-book allowance into a safety net for exactly that weakness; for the closed-book half it was strict trigger→case→ratio→apply with "one case only" discipline.
- Highest-value move: the cold, full-length timed mock. It converted diffuse confidence into a mapped list of precise, execution-level errors — every one fixable by exam day — and confirmed a 22/30 pass with the case half maxed, shifting focus onto DCFR precision and length rather than knowledge.

## PART C — ONE-LINE SUMMARY

A two-mode exam (open-book DCFR application + closed-book case recall) where the student's true bottleneck was article-number precision under fatigue, not understanding — so the method's signature adaptation was weaponising the open-book allowance itself (tabbed, colour-coded DCFR + verify-before-writing) and using cold timed mocks to expose execution errors instead of re-teaching content.
